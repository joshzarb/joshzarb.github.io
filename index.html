Group Project - The relationship between Metacritic score and video games over time and over console.¶

By Justin Levine and Joshua Zarb¶

Introduction¶

Over the past couple of years, while our group members were playing video games, we wondered that despite video game graphics and sounds improving, was the overall quality of video games also improving?

Video game consoles are often split into "generations", based on their release date and their main competitors. The 7th generation, beginning around 2005, brought several trends in video gaming: more focus on online interaction, a shift towards Western developers, and more mainstream recognition of gaming.

On the other hand, many are enthusiasts of older consoles such as the PlayStation. We wanted to examine if there was any evidence to support either preference. To explore this we used Metacritic (a website that aggregates reviews for video games) to determine how the quality of video games has changed over the years, comparing pre- and post-7th generation games.

This is a list of all consoles that have at least one game in our dataset. We can then divide this list into old and new consoles and split the dataset into two halves, one for each category.

As mentioned earlier, video game consoles are typically split into "generations". We can determine the generation each console belongs to and assign them to a category. Metacritic's earliest data is on 5th generation consoles, so 5th and 6th generation consoles will be in the "old" category.

  • 5th generation: Nintendo 64, PlayStation

  • 6th generation: Dreamcast, PlayStation 2, GameCube, Xbox, Game Boy Advance

Likewise, the 9th generation is the current generation, so consoles in the 7th to 9th generations will be in the "new" category.

  • 7th generation: PlayStation 3, Xbox 360, Wii, DS, PSP

  • 8th generation: Xbox One, Wii U, PlayStation 4, 3DS, PlayStation Vita

  • 9th generation: Switch, Xbox Series X, PlayStation 5, Stadia

The one anomaly in this list is the PC. Because "PC" is often a catch-all term that refers to any kind of Windows computer, games have consistently released on PC, even going back to the 1990s. Since the PC does not fit into any one console generation, and since it is not mainly considered a video game console, the PC will not be placed in either category.

Linear regression¶

Now we can determine just how significant these trends are. To start, we create a linear regression model for review scores, based on two interaction terms: whether or not a game's console is pre- or post-7th generation, and a game's release year.

In [ ]:
X = games[["new_console", "release_year"]]
y = games["meta_score"]
reg = sklearn.linear_model.LinearRegression().fit(X, y)
print(reg.coef_)

[-1.83092777  0.21212776]

These coefficients tell us that a game being on a new console has a negative impact on the game's score, outweighing the positive impact of a game's release date being one year later.

Calculating the p-values of each interaction term will tell us how significant each interaction term is to the score.

Conclusion¶

In summary, we wanted to tell if the overall quality of video games were improving throught the years. We did this by first obtaining the video games' Metacritic scores and creating various visualizations such as scatterplots and violinplots, comparing the video game's release date with their Metascores. Afterwards we performed a linear regression to determine how significant the trends we were seeing in these plots were. At the end the p-value we obtained was less than 0.5 suggesting that we fail to reject the null hypothesis and that there is not a strong relationship between the year that video games were released and their overall quality.

Examples
--------

    The simplest way to use nbconvert is

            > jupyter nbconvert mynotebook.ipynb

            which will convert mynotebook.ipynb to the default format (probably HTML).

            You can specify the export format with `--to`.
            Options include ['asciidoc', 'custom', 'html', 'latex', 'markdown', 'notebook', 'pdf', 'python', 'rst', 'script', 'slides'].

            > jupyter nbconvert --to latex mynotebook.ipynb

            Both HTML and LaTeX support multiple output templates. LaTeX includes
            'base', 'article' and 'report'.  HTML includes 'basic' and 'full'. You
            can specify the flavor of the format used.

            > jupyter nbconvert --to html --template basic mynotebook.ipynb

            You can also pipe the output to stdout, rather than a file

            > jupyter nbconvert mynotebook.ipynb --stdout

            PDF is generated via latex

            > jupyter nbconvert mynotebook.ipynb --to pdf

            You can get (and serve) a Reveal.js-powered slideshow

            > jupyter nbconvert myslides.ipynb --to slides --post serve

            Multiple notebooks can be given at the command line in a couple of 
            different ways:

            > jupyter nbconvert notebook*.ipynb
            > jupyter nbconvert notebook1.ipynb notebook2.ipynb

            or you can specify the notebooks list in a config file, containing::

                c.NbConvertApp.notebooks = ["my_notebook.ipynb"]

            > jupyter nbconvert --config mycfg.py

To see all available configurables, use `--help-all`.
